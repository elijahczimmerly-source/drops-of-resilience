Three approaches to spatial regridding

How climate downscaling pipelines translate coarse GCM output onto a fine-resolution grid — and why the method matters.

Method 01

Deterministic regridding Nearest neighbor & bilinear interpolation

Apply a fixed mathematical rule to fill in values between coarse grid points. No observations, no learning — just geometry.

The simplest approach: take the coarse GCM field and apply an interpolation (or resampling) rule to assign a value to every fine-resolution cell. The two most common rules are nearest neighbor, which copies the value of the closest coarse cell, and bilinear interpolation, which computes a distance-weighted average of the surrounding coarse values.

Both methods produce a smooth (or stepwise) field, but neither invents spatial detail that the GCM didn't already contain. A 25× upscaled field will be spatially bland — hilltops and valleys are invisible because the GCM never resolved them. The fine grid has more pixels, but no more information.

Why it matters for precipitation

Bilinear interpolation averages neighboring coarse cells. For temperature, that's usually fine — the field is smooth. For precipitation, it creates problems: a storm cell (20 mm) next to a dry cell (0 mm) produces boundary values of 10 mm, a physical state that neither cell actually experienced. When a quantile-mapping step follows, it maps from an artificial input value, which can distort the correction.

Bottom line: Fast and simple. Good enough when a statistical correction method (like QDM) follows and does the real work. The interpolation method is not a major lever — but for non-Gaussian fields like precipitation, nearest neighbor may slightly outperform bilinear because it preserves real GCM values.

Method 02

Constructed Analogs

Don't interpolate — search. Find historical observed days with similar large-scale patterns and blend their real fine-resolution fields.

Instead of computing values between coarse grid points, this method uses the GCM's coarse pattern as a search key into the observational archive. It finds the historical days whose large-scale patterns most closely match today's GCM output, then constructs the fine-resolution output as a weighted combination of those days' actual high-resolution observations.

The GCM's role is reduced to pattern selection: it's an index into a library of real observed spatial detail, not a source of spatial detail itself. Every fine-scale feature in the output — ridgelines, rain shadows, lake effects — traces back to a day when those features were actually measured.

Refinements: LOCA and MACA

LOCA (Localized Constructed Analogs) performs the analog search locally — each region picks its own best-matching days, so Iowa and Nebraska can draw from different historical dates in the same output. MACA (Multivariate Adaptive Constructed Analogs) adds the constraint that analog days for temperature and precipitation must be selected jointly, preserving cross-variable relationships.

Tradeoff: The method can only produce spatial patterns that actually occurred in the observational record. If climate change produces a genuinely novel pattern with no historical precedent, the method defaults to the closest available match, which may not be representative.

Method 03

Analog Regression GARD

A hybrid: find analog days like before, but instead of blending their fields, fit a regression that learns the coarse–fine relationship — then apply it to today's GCM.

Like Constructed Analogs, GARD begins by searching the observational record for days with similar large-scale patterns. But instead of directly blending those days' fine-scale fields, it uses the analog pool as training data: for each fine pixel, it fits a regression model that captures the local statistical relationship between coarse-scale predictors and fine-scale response.

When today's GCM value (coral) falls slightly beyond the observed range, the regression can still predict — it extrapolates along the fitted relationship.

Pure Constructed Analogs would be capped at the highest observed value (0.59), because it can only blend what it has seen. GARD's regression predicts 0.64 by extending the statistical trend. This matters for climate change scenarios, where GCM values may drift outside the historical envelope.

Tradeoff: Extrapolation only works for modest departures from historical experience. If the GCM produces a pattern wildly unlike anything in the record, the regression is extending a line into territory where there's no evidence it still holds.

Comparison

At a glance

Source of fine-scale detail Handles novel climates? Key assumption
Deterministic regridding Mathematical rule (geometry only) Yes — works on any GCM field Spatial smoothness between grid points
Constructed Analogs Real observed days, blended by pattern similarity No — limited to observed patterns The future resembles some combination of past days
Analog Regression Regression learned from observed analog days Slightly — can extrapolate modestly Local coarse–fine relationships are linear and stable

In practice, all three approaches are typically followed by a bias correction step (such as QDM or quantile mapping) that maps the regridded values onto observed local distributions. The regridding method determines the spatial structure of the input to that correction; the correction determines the distributional fidelity of the output.
